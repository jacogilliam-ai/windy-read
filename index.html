The read

The reach is here. The revenue path isn't.

58,800 YouTube subscribers. 5,000 on TikTok. Roblox launched. The audience showed up. Most of them watch and leave. There's no path from the view to the cart, no product that keeps a kid coming back, no engine that compounds what you've already built.

What follows is that path. Ninety days to prove the funnel, years to build the franchise, laid out as if I started tomorrow.

The character read

She talks to camera. That's rare, and it's a channel.

Most kids' IP characters live inside the story. Windy talks straight to the camera. Nothing else in kids' IP does that.

Kids trust her. Parents watch that trust form and start trusting her too. That's why she can carry a product drop, a welcome email, a Roblox reveal. She's the voice a parent is willing to hear from at checkout, in the inbox, on the subscription page. She's not a mascot. She's the trusted narrator, and the channel is already built into the show.

The footprint on her vest is already the mark. Put it on every tee, every box, and every unlock screen, and one character carries a whole wardrobe.

The site read

Three things I'd fix first.

Looked at windyweatherfoot.com from an outside perspective. Three things that would move numbers before anything on the plan below ever ships.

01

Your Pledge CTA dead-ends.

The "Download the Pledge PDF" button on your homepage links to /collections/all. Not a PDF, not gated, not the pledge product page. It sits on your most trafficked page under an unmissable heading, and every visitor who clicks it lands on a generic product grid. One link fix, and the same button starts capturing emails instead of leaking them.

02

Your character discovery is invisible to the internet.

Seven characters, one carousel URL, seven "Shop [Name]'s Items" buttons that reload the same page. Meanwhile all seven character collections already exist at their own URLs, they just aren't wired up. No character data for search engines either, so Google and AI answers have no anchor for "who is Windy Weatherfoot" queries. Wire the buttons, build a real page for each character, add character data for search. Biggest single search opportunity on the site sitting unclaimed.

03

Your post-purchase experience isn't on-brand.

Every customer who buys your product gets kicked into the default Shopify Account and Orders pages. No Weatherfoot header, no logo, no colors, no styling. The word "Weatherfoot" appears once at the top and that's the extent of the branding. They just handed you money, and the first thing they see is a screen that could belong to any online store on the internet. Fixable through Shopify customer account extensions or theme customization.

And one thing I tested myself.

Your email list captures. It just doesn't route anywhere.

I subscribed to your homepage list on July 10. Since then: zero welcome email, zero product tease, zero follow-up. The signups are landing in the default Shopify customer list, and no email platform (Klaviyo, Mailchimp, anything comparable) is connected on the back end. So there's no welcome sequence, no segmentation, no automation, and no way to speak to a subscriber differently from a buyer. Every warm lead you're paying to acquire is sitting in a plain contact list, cooling.

Why now

The window is open.

Kids' IP is going through a consolidation window. Bluey demonstrated the ceiling. Slumberkins and Little Passports proved the middle. The gap between them, character-driven and preservation-minded, is empty right now.

Windy Weatherfoot has the material for it. The next twelve months decide whether another IP fills the space first.

Repeat

The Preservation Club

A monthly subscription that turns Generation Preservation™ from a brand promise into something kids actually do at home. Every founding member gets a footprint vest, Windy's style or Walker's, in their size. Every month brings a new mission tied to that month's episodes, and a patch to earn.

The Club opens with a welcome kit: printables, digital access, first mission. Vests ship to founding members in month four, once sizing runs are through kids' apparel production.

Windy earns hers in the show. Kids earn theirs at home. Real missions parents can guide: plant something, pick up trash on a walk, help a neighbor. Finish the mission, iron the badge on. Iron-on beats velcro for durability and sew-on for effort.

The show writes the storyline. The club runs the game.

A patch a month, tied to that month's episodes.

First 90 days

A rough map, not a promise.

Bluey's site works because BBC drops finished animation straight onto every page. Claynosaurz does the same from their 3D pipeline. The advantage is owning the animation, and both companies already do. You already own yours: episodes, character stills, the shots where Windy talks to the camera. Your Shopify isn't pulling from any of it yet.

Three phases. Phase 1 is site, email, and content, run on contract. You measure the work. Phase 2 is proving the funnel through what already exists: blog, audience, printables. Phase 3 is where you invest behind what the first two have already earned.

Month 1 is a build month. Everything lands on an unpublished Shopify draft and a staged Klaviyo account. Nothing goes live until your brand team signs off. Month 2 is publish and expand. Month 3 is measure and prove what's working. Tap any item below for the detail.

Month 1

Build the foundation.

Existing Shopify theme duplicated into an unpublished draft

Your current live site keeps running exactly as it is while I work. Nothing customers see changes until you're ready to publish. Think of it as building a house next door to the one you're living in.

Klaviyo set up, connected to Shopify, sending domain authenticated

Klaviyo is what kids brands at your scale actually use for email. Right now your signups land in the default Shopify contact list, which is a contact list, not a marketing tool. Klaviyo turns them into a segmentable, automatable audience. DNS setup lets you send from hello@windyweatherfoot.com instead of a generic Klaviyo sender, which parents trust more.

Baseline metrics captured before anything ships

Before any changes go live, I snapshot site conversion, list size, blog-to-cart, and PDP views. That's the "before" picture. In Month 3, I compare it to the "after" so we can prove the work moved the numbers.

Homepage Pledge CTA rebuilt behind an email capture

Right now the button sends people to your all-products page. Nobody who clicks gets the PDF, nobody gets on your list. I rebuild it so the button opens a form, parent enters name + email, gets the PDF delivered, and joins the welcome sequence. Same button, but now it grows your list instead of leaking traffic.

Seven character content pages built, each with FictionalCharacter schema

Right now your seven characters share one carousel URL. When someone Googles "Windy Weatherfoot character," Google has nothing to show. I build a real page for each character (bio, personality, favorite episode, related products) with structured data underneath so Google and AI answer engines can finally point to them. Your biggest single search opportunity.

Character shop buttons wired to real collections

Your seven "Shop [Name]'s Items" buttons all reload the same carousel page. The character collections exist at their own URLs already. They just aren't wired up. Small fix, big deal for character-driven browsing.

Six activity PDFs gated behind Klaviyo forms

You have six free downloadable PDFs (Pledge, Pack Song, Bonjour, So Epic, Butterfly Maze, Word Search). Same file, but if we gate them behind an email form, every download becomes an email signup. Your list starts building the day this ships.

JSON-LD structured data on product, blog, and category pages

Structured data is hidden code that tells Google exactly what a page is. Without it, Google is guessing. With it, your products become eligible for Google Shopping surfacing, blog posts get rich-snippet eligibility, collections rank better. Invisible work that pays back in search traffic.

Meta titles and descriptions rewritten across the site

The title and description Google shows in search results are what people see and click on. Most of your pages have generic or missing metadata. I rewrite them to be specific, keyword-relevant, and click-worthy.

301 redirects mapped for any URLs that change

When we build new character pages or restructure content, some URLs will change. Without redirects, Google forgets the old URLs and your search rankings drop. With them, Google follows the trail and all your existing SEO carries forward.

Welcome flow drafted end to end, tested on sample subscribers

When someone signs up, they should get a series of emails introducing the world (character intros, Pledge delivery, first activity kit, first-order incentive). Right now nothing happens. I draft all the emails, wire them in Klaviyo, and test them with sample subscribers before publishing. You approve the whole flow before real signups start receiving it.

Month 2

Ship and expand.

Month 1 build published in staged batches as brand review approves

Instead of a single scary "flip the switch" moment, we publish batch by batch: character pages first, then gated PDFs, then meta updates, then the welcome flow. Each batch goes live independently. Lower risk, easier to review.

Character pages get show clips embedded as autoplay muted loops

This is where the site starts to feel like the show. Each character page gets a short clip embedded at the top, playing on loop, no sound. It's what Bluey and Claynosaurz do. Requires source video from your animation library. Without it, we lean on YouTube embeds (functional but not cinematic).

Post-purchase account experience rebranded

Right now when a customer buys, they get kicked into default Shopify Account and Orders pages. No branding, no colors, nothing. I rebuild these pages to feel like the rest of the site so buyers stay in your world at the highest-trust moment.

Blog template rebuilt with matching product and activity per post

Currently every blog post embeds the same four products regardless of topic. I rebuild the template so each post is smart. An article about home routines links to the Pledge box and the Butterfly Maze. An article about the show links to the character plush. Turns your blog from decoration into a sales channel.

Post-purchase flow drafted and staged

After someone buys, they should hear from you again in useful ways. Care instructions for the plush at day 14, matching product suggestion at day 30, invite back to the site at day 45. I build all three emails, wire them up, stage for your review.

Quarterly season flows scoped and scheduled

Four seasonal moments tied to your show's world (spring plant, summer earn-your-hat, fall harvest, winter home-by-supper). Each becomes a small flow that reactivates your list around a theme. Scoped in Month 2, sends start with the first upcoming season.

Reviews platform installed on product pages

Zero reviews on any of your seven products right now. Parents check reviews before buying anything for their kids. I install a review platform (Judge.me or similar) and set up auto-request emails so every customer gets asked to review after they receive their order.

Weekly activity email template built and running

Every week, subscribers get an email tied to that week's episode: an activity, a printable, a discussion prompt for parents. Requires knowing your episode release schedule. If no fixed schedule exists, we build around seasonal themes instead.

Month 3

Measure, hand off, expand.

Site conversion measured against Week 1 baseline

The "before" and "after" get compared. How much has your conversion rate moved? Which pages improved? Which need more work?

Email list growth measured against target

How big is the list now versus Week 1? What's the download-to-signup conversion on gated PDFs? Which entry points bring the highest-intent subscribers?

Blog posting twice a week in your voice, illustrator contracted

Consistent cadence, brand-voice writing. Illustrator makes header images so posts feel like an extension of the show, not a corporate blog.

Blog-to-cart conversion measured

How many blog readers click through to products? Which posts convert best? Feed that back into what to write next.

Documentation delivered: theme, Klaviyo, blog publishing guides

At the end of the contract, you should be able to keep everything running without me. I write plain-English guides for the theme, the flows, and the blog publishing workflow so anyone on your team can maintain them.

Regular reporting cadence with the WW team established

Monthly report with the metrics that matter, delivered via a template we agree on. Continues after the contract if you want.

Phase 2 scope conversation with WW leadership

What worked, what didn't, what to build next. Products, funded by WW. Phase 2 is where the revenue architecture starts to activate.
