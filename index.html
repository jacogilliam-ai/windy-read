The read

The reach is here. The revenue path isn't.

58,800 YouTube subscribers. 5,000 on TikTok. Roblox launched. The audience showed up. Most of them watch and leave. There's no path from the view to the cart, no product that keeps a kid coming back, no engine that compounds what you've already built.

What follows is that path. Ninety days to prove the funnel, years to build the franchise, laid out as if I started tomorrow.

The character read

She talks to camera. That's rare, and it's a channel.

Most kids' IP characters live inside the story. Windy talks straight to the camera. Nothing else in kids' IP does that.

Kids trust her. Parents watch that trust form and start trusting her too. That's why she can carry a product drop, a welcome email, a Roblox reveal. She's the voice a parent is willing to hear from at checkout, in the inbox, on the subscription page. She's not a mascot. She's the trusted narrator, and the channel is already built into the show.

The footprint on her vest is already the mark. Put it on every tee, every box, and every unlock screen, and one character carries a whole wardrobe.

The site read

Three things I'd fix first.

Looked at windyweatherfoot.com from an outside perspective. Three things that would move numbers before anything on the plan below ever ships.

01

Your Pledge CTA dead-ends.

The "Download the Pledge PDF" button on your homepage links to /collections/all. Not a PDF, not gated, not the pledge product page. It sits on your most trafficked page under an unmissable heading, and every visitor who clicks it lands on a generic product grid. One link fix, and the same button starts capturing emails instead of leaking them.

02

Your character discovery is invisible to the internet.

Seven characters, one carousel URL, seven "Shop [Name]'s Items" buttons that reload the same page. Meanwhile all seven character collections already exist at their own URLs, they just aren't wired up. No character data for search engines either, so Google and AI answers have no anchor for "who is Windy Weatherfoot" queries. Wire the buttons, build a real page for each character, add character data for search. Biggest single search opportunity on the site sitting unclaimed.

03

Your post-purchase experience isn't on-brand.

Every customer who buys your product gets kicked into the default Shopify Account and Orders pages. No Weatherfoot header, no logo, no colors, no styling. The word "Weatherfoot" appears once at the top and that's the extent of the branding. They just handed you money, and the first thing they see is a screen that could belong to any online store on the internet. Fixable through Shopify customer account extensions or theme customization.

And one thing I tested myself.

Your email list captures. It just doesn't route anywhere.

I subscribed to your homepage list on July 10. Since then: zero welcome email, zero product tease, zero follow-up. The signups are landing in the default Shopify customer list, and no email platform (Klaviyo, Mailchimp, anything comparable) is connected on the back end. So there's no welcome sequence, no segmentation, no automation, and no way to speak to a subscriber differently from a buyer. Every warm lead you're paying to acquire is sitting in a plain contact list, cooling.

Why now

The window is open.

Kids' IP is going through a consolidation window. Bluey demonstrated the ceiling. Slumberkins and Little Passports proved the middle. The gap between them, character-driven and preservation-minded, is empty right now.

Windy Weatherfoot has the material for it. The next twelve months decide whether another IP fills the space first.

The product read

Your best SKUs already tell you what belongs in the lineup.

The lucky purple comb and the pledge box come from moments in the show. The instinct is right. Kids ask for what they saw a character use. Plush, bag charms, the vest, and character apparel are the next of that kind, and they're the ones kids ask for by name.

Put story-first products at the front of the lineup, and each one pulls from an audience the show already has. Here's what that looks like.

The products

Things kids collect, repeat, and unlock.

Every product does one of three jobs. It builds a collection, it comes back every month, or it unlocks something in the game. Same catalog, three doors in for ages 2 to 10. Kids age up inside the world, not out of it.

This is the Year 1 to 3 vision, not the 90-day scope. Phase 1 is site plus email. Everything below is what gets built once Phase 1 has earned it.

Collect

Blind-box bag charms

Clip-on characters that ride to school and back. A surprise in every pack, a full cast to chase, and the whole thing lives where kids show each other what they love: the backpack strap.

Wave one ships as soft plush charms in Q2, same factory profile as the plush line. Wave two upgrades to vinyl blind-box figurines once volume justifies the tooling. The reason a kid asks for the next one.

Repeat

The Preservation Club

A monthly subscription that turns Generation Preservation™ from a brand promise into something kids actually do at home. Every founding member gets a footprint vest, Windy's style or Walker's, in their size. Every month brings a new mission tied to that month's episodes, and a patch to earn.

The Club opens with a welcome kit: printables, digital access, first mission. Vests ship to founding members in month four, once sizing runs are through kids' apparel production.

Windy earns hers in the show. Kids earn theirs at home. Real missions parents can guide: plant something, pick up trash on a walk, help a neighbor. Finish the mission, iron the badge on. Iron-on beats velcro for durability and sew-on for effort.

The show writes the storyline. The club runs the game.

A patch a month, tied to that month's episodes.

Revenue architecture

Four revenue types. One IP.

Owned

You make it, you sell it.

  • Plush line
  • Kids apparel
  • Books and activity
  • Bag charms

Recurring

Locked-in monthly income.

  • Preservation Club (monthly subscription)
  • Roblox game pass (in-game monthly)
  • Founders' Ring (annual exclusives tier)

Royalty

Someone else pays for the play.

  • Roblox unlock economy
  • Book royalties
  • Brand licensing (bedding, backpacks, snacks, party)

Creator

Other people grow the brand for you.

  • Anchor partners at Ryan's World scale
  • Vetted mid-tier creators
  • 10 to 15 percent affiliate commission
  • Cross-IP collabs (Year 2, long deal cycle)

The show is the anchor. Everything else is category expansion.

The engine

The footprint ties every piece together, from her vest to the unlock screen. The Generation Preservation Club is the monthly subscription that keeps all four spinning between episodes.

Each turn, more kids arrive, the fandom runs deeper, and the creator roster grows. So the next turn reaches more kids for less. That is the difference between a store and a franchise. A store sells once. A machine compounds.

The delight play

Cheap surprises, real fans.

One flat envelope, once a quarter, to the top customers. A scavenger hunt card for the backyard. A seed packet with a note from Chip. A recipe card for Chip's favorite snack. Ships for under a dollar, cheaper than a single Meta ad, and the kid remembers Windy Weatherfoot every time they use it.

Email keeps them warm. Envelopes make them show up.

First 90 days

A rough map, not a promise.

Bluey's site works because BBC drops finished animation straight onto every page. Claynosaurz does the same from their 3D pipeline. The advantage is owning the animation, and both companies already do. You already own yours: episodes, character stills, the shots where Windy talks to the camera. Your Shopify isn't pulling from any of it yet.

Three phases. Phase 1 is site, email, and content, run on contract. You measure the work. Phase 2 is proving the funnel through what already exists: blog, audience, printables. Phase 3 is where you invest behind what the first two have already earned.

Building toward a cinematic, video-forward storefront takes six months, not ninety days. The goal is a Shopify that carries the show, not just the products. It needs access to your existing animation library. That's a Phase 2 conversation, not a Month 1 promise.

One caveat: I've worked from public research only. Where the plan depends on your team's capacity or current production load, I've made calls from the outside. Happy to be corrected.

Month 1

Site + funnel.

  • Homepage hero CTA fixed (Pledge PDF gated + wired correctly)
  • Character shop buttons wired to real collections
  • Per-character content pages built (7 URLs, each with FictionalCharacter schema)
  • Activity downloads gated behind email
  • JSON-LD structured data on product, blog, and category pages
  • Meta titles + descriptions rewritten across the site
  • Welcome flow live (currently absent, per live test): cast intros, pledge download, first activity kit, first-order discount
  • Weekly activity email tied to that week's episode

Month 2

Video-forward, blog-forward, post-purchase.

  • Character pages rebuilt with show clips (existing YouTube masters, embedded as autoplay muted loops)
  • Post-purchase account experience rebranded (Weatherfoot header, styling, order tracking)
  • Blog: every post links to a matching product and activity at the bottom
  • Music cross-linking: YouTube descriptions to Spotify, tracks in welcome flow
  • Post-purchase flow live: care tips week two, matching product week four, next-invite week six
  • Quarterly season flows scheduled: spring plant, summer earn-your-hat, fall harvest, winter home-by-supper

Month 3

Measure and expand.

  • Site conversion rate measured against baseline
  • Email list growth measured (target set by activity download rate)
  • Blog posting twice a week in your voice, illustrator contracted for headers
  • Blog-to-cart conversion measured
  • Regular reporting to the WW team
  • Conversation about Phase 2 (products, funded by WW)
